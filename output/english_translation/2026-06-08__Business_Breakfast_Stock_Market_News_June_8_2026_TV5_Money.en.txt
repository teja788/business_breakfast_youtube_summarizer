Good morning and welcome to the Business Breakfast show presented by LIC. A fresh trading week — and a fresh trading day — is about to begin. This Monday morning the markets are looking very, very weak. Across Asian markets the screens are all in the red zone — Korea, Japan and especially Taiwan are all trading with losses, and over the weekend US markets also closed lower. So this morning we are facing a double whammy. One is the geopolitical crisis: with strikes and counter-strikes, war is again underway between Iran and Israel. This time Iran fired missiles directly at Israel, saying that because Israel was striking Beirut, the capital of Lebanon, "we will strike you." Neither side is backing down. Meanwhile Donald Trump is telling Israeli PM Netanyahu not to escalate against Iran; in a channel interview Trump said "I call the shots, he doesn't" — referring to Netanyahu. US officials also distanced themselves from Israel's strikes on Beirut, saying they did not approve them. Overall the West Asia crisis is not weakening at all — it keeps strengthening.

The second factor is an AI sell-off across Asian markets. On Friday the Nasdaq fell 4% — its worst one-day fall since April 2025, almost 14 months. Is this a healthy reset or a warning shot? We'll get more clarity this week. Most people think it's a healthy correction because valuations had run up enormously. For example, the S&P 500 is now dominated more than 35% by AI stocks, and the index trades about 75% above its 200-day moving average — that shows how big the bull run has been. So a correction from here was due at some point; whether it began Friday or continues is the question. The S&P 500 fell 2.6% Friday, but if you strip out the AI stocks the index was essentially flat — meaning the sell-off was only in AI names. Now AI/semiconductor-dominated markets like Korea and Taiwan are seeing selling. In Korea, Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix together make up about 50% of the Kospi; in Taiwan, TSMC alone is more than 40% of the index — and selling has now arrived in these stocks. At one stage the Korean index was down 8.5% near its lower circuit, then recovered to about a 7% loss. So this is a reversal in AI stocks. The sell-off that hit seven-eight-nine stocks in the US is now visible in AI-related stocks in Korea and Taiwan too. So it is a double whammy: West Asia crisis plus an AI-trade reversal. Gift Nifty points to an opening with losses of more than 250 points. Will this Monday be a "Black Monday" with selling across all sectors here too, or only in the few semiconductor-related names we have (Netweb Technologies, ATA/AeroFlex-type names, MosChip a semiconductor stock, Kaynes Technologies which the market also treats as a semi stock)?

On the banks: on Friday the RBI made a major effort in its policy to rescue the falling rupee. It gave tax relief to foreign portfolio investors and took several macro-economic reinforcement measures to attract foreign capital and maintain our growth trajectory. Over the next three-four months these could bring roughly 65-70 billion dollars into our markets. Our current account deficit as of 2026 is 75 billion dollars; the EFCNR/ECB and other relaxations, plus forex swaps allowed to PSU banks (forex swap facility extended to September 30), are meant to attract dollars and fill that gap. Against that backdrop banks and financials should trade somewhat positive; without all this turmoil there would have been a good rally in Bank Nifty today, but with the weak openings, the global corrections, the geopolitical crisis and crude back at 96 dollars, bank stocks may not move much today. Other sectors will see selling — metals, for instance, because metal/commodity prices fell 2-4% over the weekend, hitting Hindustan Copper, Hindalco, Hindustan Zinc, Nalco. Power stocks may also see some selling because their recent rise came partly from domestic demand and partly from the data-centre theme.

Will Indian markets perform if the AI trade reverses? We always said we underperformed because we lack such AI companies. If the AI trade reverses, will that money flow into Indian markets, and can our under-performance reduce? Right now we are not in a position to outperform, but the under-performance may ease. We also need to watch whether IT stocks see buying, because if the AI trade reverses, institutional investors may turn back toward traditional IT. FIIs are continuing to sell — on Friday they sold 8,776 crore worth of stocks, while DIIs bought even more at 9,134 crore (about 400 crore extra buying). The rupee recovered well on Friday on RBI's measures, coming to 94.94/94 against the dollar after going almost to 97. Forex experts say the rupee could trade below 95 up to September, which would be good for markets and for our weakening currency. Conditions are changing very fast, so this is a time to be careful — no complacency, no "it'll recover anyway" attitude. Indices like the Nasdaq are falling 4-5% in a single day; the Kospi fell 8% and touched its lower circuit today.

[Conversation with analyst Kutumba Rao]

Kutumba Rao: The chances of further weakness look higher to me. The correction in the US that has finally set in after a very long time — whether in the Magnificent Seven or the Nvidia-led AI stocks — looks like it could be severe. Yes, last time it corrected like this and then climbed back to new highs, so we've seen that too. But I'm also thinking differently: history shows how markets correct after a large IPO. Now there's the SpaceX IPO. By past patterns in the US, the S&P fell 38% around the Visa IPO in 2008, and the Shanghai index fell about 22% in 2010 when Agricultural Bank of China did its ~22 billion dollar IPO. So if markets hold after the SpaceX IPO it would be truly surprising. Whether a natural correction is setting in, and whether it set in a day early this time, is the doubt. Mega IPOs anywhere in the world impact capital markets, and since America is the largest economy and biggest capital market, when it catches a cold the whole world catches it — corrections can set in elsewhere too. We see the indication in the Korean market and other Asian markets trading very weak today; even the Japanese market is down 4% already this morning.

On the "big-bang reform" — the concession to FIIs to invest in the debt market: I think it was a very, very bad decision. In India interest rates are high, so you pay reverse interest on the money coming in, plus they're being let off tax-free on gilts and bonds. What about domestic investors and domestic funds — couldn't you give them some concession on debt too? It's the domestic institutions and domestic investors who have supported the markets all this while. You looked only at FII outflows — and not even capital-market outflows, only debt-market — and gave concessions to the debt market. So I felt the RBI move will be extremely short-lived and may not have a big impact. The rupee's biggest move in a couple of months — strengthening Friday — could fizzle out, and I wouldn't be surprised if it goes back to 96-97 within one or two months. It felt to me like a knee-jerk reaction rather than a well-thought-out, big-picture decision. We'll see how things pan out; I may be right or wrong. As of now the Indian debt market also lacks depth; yes, they'll include Indian debt in global indices and say flows will increase, but when a currency is depreciating, investors won't invest even with high interest rates — remember that on a net basis.

Overall, if you look at our markets, the strength is in the broader market more than the front line. A simple statistic shows how silently the bull run has been going: before March this year, of the 3,343 BSE-listed companies with 10,000-crore market cap and below, only 12% (398) were positive; after April, now 70% (2,358 stocks) have turned positive — that shows how mega the rally has been since April, since our last workshop in Hyderabad. In large caps, where institutional influence is high, retail investors are ignoring some stocks; and clearly, wherever earnings were good, investors rewarded those stocks. This is not purely a liquidity-driven rally — market breadth improved sharply with delivery-based buying, good earnings, improving risk appetite and investment in select new-generation stocks. I think this recovery in sub-10,000-crore market-cap companies is likely to continue, and select stocks that correct off and on could move to the mid-cap range over the next year. So stock picking is the most important thing from here.
