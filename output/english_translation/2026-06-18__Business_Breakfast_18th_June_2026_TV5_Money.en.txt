GLOBAL CUES
After a gap of 16 months, the top leaders of the two countries met yesterday —
Prime Minister Modi and US President Donald Trump met on the sidelines of the
summit in France. Nothing of great significance came out of the meeting itself;
the whole backdrop revolved around the G7 and the Iran–US deal, those two
issues. But as far as India is concerned, Trump struck a very positive note —
"India is a great friend of mine," and Modi now looks "like an angel" to him.
He used two words about Modi — an angel, and a "killer" / a very tough
negotiator. Using "angel" and "killer" in equal measure is something only
Donald Trump can pull off.

Anyway, the ceasefire everyone had been waiting for was once again announced
officially, from Trump's own mouth yesterday — an MoU was signed. On behalf of
Iran and the US, Donald Trump himself signed it. These were all very dramatic
developments, but the actual agreement will be formalised tomorrow. Only after
that will we see how the situation truly shapes up. At one stage — about three
months ago — relations between us and America had soured so badly that there was
even fear we might turn into enemy nations. But seeing yesterday's visuals again,
it was a feast for the eyes.

So that's the global picture. Yesterday Kevin Warsh — who chaired his very first
Fed (FOMC) meeting as chairperson — made hawkish comments. There was an
indication that there will be rate hikes in 2026, at least one rate hike for
sure. The inflation outlook is still tough. And easing — i.e. interest-rate cuts
— is not going to happen any time soon. All of this came out of yesterday's FOMC
meeting. Of the 18 Fed officials, nine are saying there will be a rate hike in
2026; 2027 is still unclear. Notably, Kevin Warsh is not among those nine —
because he is the person Donald Trump appointed. Trump abused Jerome Powell
repeatedly and brought in Kevin Warsh, who, on arriving, said he would cut rates.
But under current conditions — the inflationary conditions brought on by the war
— cutting rates is not so easy. Kevin Warsh has understood this, and so has
Donald Trump. As long as Warsh sits in that chair, he too has stopped talking
about cuts. Once you sit in that seat, no one can do much else; only after you
get there do the realities fully sink in.

So US money markets are expecting a rate hike maybe in October — a hike of 40 to
50 basis points, i.e. roughly a 0.5% rise in rates is possible. As a result, the
10-year bond yield rose to almost 4.5% yesterday, and the Dollar Index again
crossed 100. Equity markets, meanwhile, closed negative — especially in tech
stocks; selling was seen in names like Nvidia. Overall the hyperscaler stocks all
traded and closed with losses; the Nasdaq ended in the red.

So markets are once again focusing heavily on capex-related themes. There's a
historic $700-billion-plus capex cycle under way — as we showed clearly in a good
slide in our recent workshop presentation, covering how much each company is
going to invest. Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta, Oracle — all of them are now running
the biggest capex cycle, $700-billion-plus of capital expenditure, all of it on
infrastructure: the advanced GPUs they take from Nvidia, and data-centre
expansions. Now the question of how much of this is debt versus equity is
starting to show up again in US equity markets. So for some time, the "AI trade"
sentiment could turn somewhat negative. Crude (Brent) is trading around $78.28 at
this hour.

INDIAN MARKETS

with SEBI this very week — a ~Rs 38,000-crore IPO is being readied. The
announcement related to this will come out tomorrow, guaranteed, and the markets
are waiting for it.

Also, the NSE IPO filing is done. Yesterday — they're likely looking to raise
around Rs 30,000 crore; the exact figure isn't known, but the market estimate is
about Rs 30,000 crore. That would be the largest IPO so far: Hyundai came in 2024
at Rs 27,859 crore, which we thought was the highest; before that LIC at
Rs 20,557 crore, Paytm at Rs 18,300 crore, Tata Capital at Rs 15,500 crore, Coal
India at Rs 15,200 crore. Compared to all of those, the NSE IPO will be very

paper supply is about to enter the market.

Along with that, another piece of news in today's Economic Times: about $26
billion worth of shares of companies that had earlier done IPOs are about to be
released from their lock-in period. Over the next one month alone, roughly $16
billion worth of paper/stock will be released — relating to 31 companies — and
after that another $10–11 billion. Overall, will these lock-in releases and IPOs
have any impact on liquidity? Because our markets are now liquidity-driven —
retail money is going in, and DIIs are investing in stocks. So will this affect
the market's trajectory? Here's the thing: if too many people believe it will
have an impact, it won't. Markets never move according to the majority view —
they don't follow what the majority thinks or doesn't think. In the US, many
expected markets to crash after the SpaceX IPO; because the majority said so, it
didn't happen. Such a situation tends to show up in markets, so let's see how the
impact of these IPOs plays out this time.

Our markets, meanwhile, coolly recaptured a $5-trillion market cap again
yesterday. Thanks to last week's low, the Nifty has risen 1,000 points and the
Bank Nifty around 4,000-plus points, which is why we can see the $5-trillion mark
again. Indian markets are now the sixth-largest market cap in the world. South
Korea has moved just ahead of us, helped by stocks like Samsung Electronics and
SK Hynix. The Taiwanese market is still above us at $5.55 trillion, so maybe if
the AI/semiconductor rally takes a breather, we could overtake Taiwan and move
ahead. These things will keep shifting back and forth — we'll keep swapping the
5th, 6th and 7th spots with South Korea and Taiwan. This trend may continue for a
while.

But it's a valuations game — because in mid- and small-caps we don't pay much
attention to valuations, since it's mostly retail investors playing there, and
retail investors always give more importance to narratives and stories than to
valuations. "Such-and-such company is supposedly going to do such-and-such, so we
must grab this stock early" — that kind of eagerness is high among retail
investors. Institutional investors, on the other hand, watch PE ratios and
price-to-book carefully before moving forward. At 70x or 100x PE, they don't come
in quickly — they'll come only if they have great faith in the company's business
model. Retail investors, for now, set the ratio aside and believe the story. That
happens often in the market. Recently too we've seen many companies — Inox
[Wind], Sterlite Technologies, HFCL, Apar, etc. — none of these are cheap stocks.
They're expensive stocks, but if you look at their PE, discounted cash flows, or
EV/EBITDA, no one would buy them. Take HFCL, for instance — in telecom infra
they're moving aggressively; data centres, the telecom sector, even aerospace —
that company's name is heard often lately. Retail investors follow whatever growth
story exists. So there's a big difference between these two spaces: the large-caps
with institutional play, and the mid/small-cap space with heavy retail play. We
should watch this carefully — don't get swept up in the hype/frenzy, but don't be
too skeptical either. If you sit out saying "it's at 100 PE, I won't buy," it can
sometimes go even to 200 PE. Such situations keep showing up. Stock-specifically,
there's plenty of action.

Now that the NSE IPO has come, the BSE stock will be in the limelight for a few
days. Also, in the NSE IPO, SBI, [SBI Caps], etc. are all selling stakes — but
LIC says it won't sell; LIC is not participating in the offer-for-sale. We should
note that too. And in [Aragen / YM] Biosciences there's a big block deal today —
promoters are selling their stake at Rs 715 per share.
